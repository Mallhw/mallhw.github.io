---
title: The problems with overanalyzing decisions
date: 2026-05-14
---

Our interpretation of people's decisions must leave leeway for different motivations for choosing those decisions. We try to understand people's motivations because we belive we can get insight into their behaviors and actions. However, it is almost impossible that one singular factor is the driving factor behind actions. Instead its like there are 4-5 major reasons why people choose to *act* in a certain way. 

Because of this in order to predict/analayze behavior a probablistic model must be taken into account. Using prior experience, we can say a person might be indulging on sweets maybe because they are hungry or because they are nervous. It is often the case that many decisions are mixtures between many motivations. So when we assume a person acts a certain way because of 80% of X and 20% of Y, we leave some room for error that one factor might be larger than the other 40% of X and 60% of Y could be possible. However when extrapolating behaviors, we must assume these axioms are true otherwise the underlying branches will divulge even further. Like a decision tree, we have to fix the nodes so we can interpret further decisions based on our assumptoin on previous behavior. However, as we go deeper into the decision tree into interpreting even more possible actions our error compounds with the previous error. Each new assumption of each new behavior must take into account the previous one.

This can be fixed with longer samples, causing the error to decrease from potentially 20% to 1%. But, given small samples of short interactions, this is impossible to truly to do with every person.
