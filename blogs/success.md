date: 2026-04-17

*Written after a talk with my coach. Post-fencing practice is when I feel the most like myself*

I've been reflecting on my highschool career and it seems like an amazing success - I've been posted on a Washington Post for an amazing fencing result, got into Stanford (my dream school), won at ISEF, fenced with oylmpians, grew a coding club, been with incredible friends, bonded over small stuff with my family, cooked better... on and on. Yet, I don't feel any sense of wonderment about myself. In fact, I feel completely like myself with nothing really changed. Except something did change.

Perhaps it's because all of these achievments came in incredibly rapid successsion. The past two years have been amazing. But, I realized that I've fundamentally changed as a person and the reason for that is because of fencing. Before the Washington Post. Before the 2nd place at Division 1. I was struggling with fencing. I didn't win any regional tournaments. I couldn't grasp new teqniques. I felt like I was sinking without a way out, and I remember saying to my coach that "I had a glass ceiling." What I didn't realize at the time was that I believed I was talentless. In summary, I was ranked 90th nationally, I didn't improve in over 3 years, and I was falling behind my peers. That was possibly the hardest period of my life. I wanted to quit at many different points. But, I didn't. That decision was the best one I've ever made because it changed my perspective. 

In fencing, losing is pretty much a gurantee. Even the best fencers lose to worser ones and evantually only 1 person can be first place. I have lost so many times. To small little kids. To people I should have beaten with certanity. To Olympians who destroyed me. That has desensitized me to loss.

There is something core that I will never forget about fencing. It's changed who I am.
